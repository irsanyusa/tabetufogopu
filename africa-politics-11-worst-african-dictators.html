11 worst African dictators | Business Insider Africa

Posted by Jenniffer Sheldon on Wednesday, June 19, 2024

Dictatorships are characterised primarily by the violation of basic human rights and a lack of freedoms for its citizens.

In Africa, many nations are enduring or have been forced to endure dictatorships.

Here are ten of Africa’s most notorious dictators.

ADVERTISEMENT

Abdel Fattah el-Sisi | Egypt | 2013- present

Sisi overthrew the country’s elected president, Mohammed Morsi, in a military coup in 2013.

He gunned down 813 peaceful protestors of his presidency and has imprisoned 40,000+ dissidents.

He has been accused of gross human rights abuses and plunging the country into further economic turmoil.

Robert Mugabe | Zimbabwe| 1987- present

ADVERTISEMENT

He is the oldest serving president in the world at 93 years old.

After Zimbabwe was declared a one party state, Zimbabwe’s parliament amended the constitution and declared Mugabe executive president meaning he was head of state, head of government, and commander-in-chief of the armed forces.

Over the years, Mugabe’s nationalist and Lenin-Marxist policies led to the sharp economic decline of Zimbabwe. His rule has led to high rates of unemployment- up to 80%, low life expectancy, hyperinflation making the Zimbabwean currency useless and the collapse of local manufacturing industry.

Eduardo Jose Dos Santos| Angola| 1979- 2017

Father to Africa’s richest woman, dos Santos came to office when the country's first post-independence president, Agostinho Neto, died, inheriting the civil war with the Unita party.

ADVERTISEMENT

He is credited with leading the country to recovery after the end of its 27-year civil war in 2002 and turning the economy into one of the world’s fastest growing economies.

However, he was accused of authoritarianism, corruption, misuse of public funding, human rights abuses and repression of political opposition.

Paul Biya| Cameroon| 1982- present

He became president after a power struggle in 1982.

Biya has all-encompassing executive and legislative powers. Biya stages sham elections every few years to justify his reign and even paid for his own electoral observers (6 ex-US congressmen) to declare the elections credible.

ADVERTISEMENT

Footage has emerged since early 2017 where it appears that a state sanctioned genocide is occurring in Southern Cameroon.

There have been reports of massive arrests, use of military tribunals to try civilians, inhumane treatment and murder.

Omar al Bashir| Sudan| 1989- present

Bashir took power in a military coup in 1989.

There is currently an International Criminal Court warrant out for his arrest on charges of genocide, war crimes, murder, rape, torture and crimes that occurred during the war of Darfur.

ADVERTISEMENT

Bashir is also accused of the oppression of non-Arab tribes residing in the Sudan.

Hissene Habre| Chad| 1982-1990

Habre seized power in 1982 from newly elected Goukuni Oueddei.

He then lost power to Idriss Deby in 1990.

He fled to Senegal when insurgents marched into Chad’s capital, N’Djamena.

ADVERTISEMENT

Habre is responsible for 40,000+ political murders and 200,000 records of torture.

Idriss Deby| Chad| 1990- present

Deby sacked the incumbent president using Libyan and Sudanese insurgents.

He has been re-elected every five years.

Deby was accused of using famine relief funds to purchase weapons and strengthen Chad’s military.

ADVERTISEMENT

Charles Taylor| Liberia| 1997- 2003

The former Liberian president is currently serving a fifty year sentence for what was recorded as the most brutal war crimes in human history.

He was charged with acts of terrorism, unlawful killings, murder, forced labour and torture.

Taylor gained the presidency after running for elections in 1997 after the end of the 1996 civil war. Taylor won by a 75% landslide, having taken over state radio and using the fear that he could restart the war should he not win.

He was alleged to have been involved in Sierra Leone’s civil war, trading weapons for blood diamonds. He was charged with aiding and abetting Sierra Leone’s rebel forces atrocities against civilians, which left many thousands dead, with people abducted and tortured. He was also accused of assisting the recruitment of child soldiers.

ADVERTISEMENT

Siad Barre| Somalia| 1969- 1991

He took power in a coup d’etat in 1969 and was overthrown in1991while attempting to retake Mogadishu in a military showdown. He was defeated by General Mohamed Farrah Aidid's army.

Barre left the country without central rule and plunging Somalia into a debilitating civil war that went on for more than 20 years.

Barre’s rule was characterised by persecution, jailing and torture of political opponents and dissidents. It is estimated by human rights’ groups that 50,000 to 60,000 people were killed from 1988 to 1989.

Hastings Kamuzu Banda| Malawi| 1963-1994

ADVERTISEMENT

Banda lost power in 1994 after he had to go for emergency brain surgery in 1993 in South Africa.

He left a legacy of poverty, starvation, repressed opposition and is responsible for the deaths of an estimated 6,000 people and thousands of torture and wrongful imprisonment victims.

Idi Amin Dada| Uganda| 1971- 1979

Amin seized power in a military coup in January 1971 deposing Milton Obote.

He was rumoured to be a cannibal, had frightening records of human rights abuse, extra judicial killings, squandering of public funds, repressing the opposition.

ADVERTISEMENT

The death toll under his 8 year rule was estimated to be at least 500,000.
